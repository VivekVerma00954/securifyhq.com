How to Choose CCTV Cameras for Professional Monitoring in NZ

Not all cameras work the same way with professional monitoring. Here's what NZ business owners need to know before buying cameras for remote CCTV monitoring.

← Back to Blog

One of the most common questions we receive from NZ businesses considering remote monitoring is: "Do I need new cameras?" In most cases, the answer is no — if your cameras support RTSP streaming, we can connect to them. But if you are buying cameras, or if you're assessing whether your existing setup is capable, there are specific technical requirements that matter a great deal for professional monitoring to work properly.

This guide covers the key factors to consider, brands that work reliably, and what to avoid if you want cameras that integrate cleanly with a remote monitoring service.

The Single Most Important Factor: RTSP Support

RTSP stands for Real Time Streaming Protocol. It's the standard method by which IP cameras expose their video feed to external systems — including monitoring platforms like SecurifyHQ. If a camera doesn't support RTSP, there is no standard way for a monitoring system to access its feed without going through the manufacturer's proprietary cloud service, which creates significant limitations.

Most professional-grade IP cameras support RTSP by default. It's typically disabled on consumer cameras that are designed to route everything through the vendor's cloud app. Before purchasing any camera, confirm RTSP support is available and that you can retrieve the stream URL from the camera's settings.

Resolution: 1080p Is the Minimum

For professional monitoring purposes, 1080p (Full HD) is the minimum acceptable resolution. This ensures that operators can identify persons and vehicles clearly enough to make informed decisions about whether an event is genuine and what action to take. Lower-resolution cameras produce footage that is technically "motion detected" but practically useless for verification — a grainy silhouette at 720p tells an operator very little.

For sites where identification of individuals is important for insurance or police purposes, 4MP or 4K cameras at key entry points are worth the investment. Wide-angle overview cameras can operate at 1080p, but close-up entry cameras benefit from higher resolution.

Night Vision and Low-Light Performance

The majority of commercial break-ins in New Zealand occur at night or in low-light conditions — exactly when camera performance matters most. For monitoring purposes, usable night-vision footage is essential. There are two main technologies to consider:

  • Infrared (IR) night vision — produces monochrome footage but works in complete darkness. Effective range varies by camera; budget for at least 20–30 metres for external cameras on commercial sites.
  • Full-colour night vision — requires some ambient light (street lighting, perimeter lighting) but produces colour footage that is more useful for identification. Hikvision ColorVu and Dahua Full-Color are well-regarded options in this category.

Avoid cameras with weak IR LEDs that wash out at close range or fail to illuminate at the distances your site requires. Always check the manufacturer's stated IR range against your actual site dimensions.

Wired vs. Wireless Cameras

For professional monitoring, wired cameras are strongly preferred over wireless. The reasons are practical rather than ideological:

  • Wired cameras (PoE — Power over Ethernet) receive both power and data through a single cable, eliminating battery concerns and Wi-Fi reliability issues
  • Wireless cameras are susceptible to signal interference, Wi-Fi dead spots, and deliberate jamming — a known technique used by sophisticated intruders
  • Connection stability is critical for monitoring; a camera that drops offline during an incident provides no value at precisely the moment it's needed

If wired installation isn't possible on parts of your site, 4G-connected cameras are a more reliable wireless alternative than Wi-Fi-dependent models. SecurifyHQ monitors both wired and cellular-connected cameras — see our full camera compatibility guide for details.

Local NVR vs. Cloud-Only Storage

Cameras that store footage exclusively in the manufacturer's cloud create a dependency on that vendor's service, their pricing, and their continued operation. For professional monitoring, footage needs to be accessible to the monitoring platform in real time — not buffered through a proprietary cloud service with artificial latency or access restrictions.

A local NVR (Network Video Recorder) paired with RTSP cameras gives you full control of your footage and provides the most reliable integration with monitoring platforms. Cameras with onboard SD card storage as a backup are also useful for redundancy, but should not be the primary storage method for a monitored site.

Brands That Work Well for Professional Monitoring

Recommended brands

  • Hikvision — industry standard, reliable RTSP, excellent NVR ecosystem, wide range of models including ColorVu for colour night vision
  • Dahua — comparable to Hikvision, strong RTSP support, good value at mid-range price points
  • Reolink — excellent value for money, reliable RTSP on most models, PoE range is solid for smaller commercial sites
  • Axis — premium build quality and software, industry-leading in reliability, higher price point suited to larger enterprise deployments
  • Uniview — strong mid-market option with good RTSP compatibility and competitive pricing in NZ

What to avoid

  • Closed cloud-only cameras — some Arlo models, Nest cameras, and similar consumer devices route everything through the vendor's cloud and do not support RTSP, making professional monitoring integration impossible or unreliable
  • No-brand imports — cheap cameras from unknown manufacturers often claim RTSP support but have unstable firmware and unreliable stream output that drops frequently
  • Outdated analogue cameras without an IP NVR — pure analogue cameras cannot stream RTSP; however, if you have an IP NVR that encodes analogue feeds digitally, those can often be monitored via the NVR's RTSP stream

What to Check Before You Buy

Before purchasing cameras for a monitored site, confirm the following with your supplier or by checking the manufacturer's documentation:

  • RTSP stream URL format is documented and accessible
  • The camera supports at least 1080p resolution at the required frame rate (minimum 15fps for monitoring purposes)
  • Night vision range is sufficient for the distances your site requires
  • PoE power is supported (for wired installations)
  • The camera can be accessed over your local network without requiring the vendor's cloud app
