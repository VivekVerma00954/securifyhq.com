How to Protect Your Construction Site From After-Hours Theft in New Zealand

Construction site theft is a persistent problem across New Zealand. Tools disappear overnight, copper wiring gets stripped, plant equipment is damaged or moved without authorisation, and materials stockpiled for the following day's work are gone by morning. For many contractors and project managers, it's not a matter of if it will happen — it's when.

This guide covers why sites get targeted, what doesn't work, and what security measures are worth investing in.

Why Construction Sites Are Targeted

Several factors make construction sites attractive to theft:

  • High-value, portable items — power tools, cables, scaffolding components, copper, and fuel are all easily resold
  • Low foot traffic after hours — sites are genuinely empty for 12+ hours on weekdays, and longer over weekends
  • Incomplete perimeters — construction sites are works in progress; fencing is often temporary and access points shift as the build progresses
  • Limited lighting — many sites have minimal external lighting, particularly in early or mid-build stages
  • Plant equipment — excavators, skid steers, and trailers left on-site overnight represent significant exposure

These aren't unique to any region — they're structural features of construction sites everywhere in NZ.

What Doesn't Work

Padlocks and Fencing Alone

Temporary fencing and padlocked containers slow people down but don't stop determined theft. A well-equipped team can remove a container padlock or cut through temporary fencing quickly. Physical barriers are necessary but not sufficient.

Basic Alarms With No Follow-Up

A siren alarm that goes off in the middle of the night on an empty site may alert nobody useful. Neighbours who hear it repeatedly stop paying attention. If there's no verified escalation path — no one who receives the alert and takes action — the alarm is largely performative.

What Does Work

Good Perimeter Lighting

Motion-activated lighting at key access points is one of the most cost-effective deterrents available. Well-lit sites are less attractive to opportunistic theft. Lighting also significantly improves camera image quality, which matters if footage is ever needed for a police report or insurance claim.

Quality IP Cameras With RTSP Support

For construction sites without fixed internet, 4G solar cameras are the practical solution. They don't require cabled power or a physical network connection. However, this is critical: not all 4G solar cameras support RTSP.

Important: Many 4G solar cameras sold in NZ are cloud-only devices. They send footage to a vendor app and cannot be connected to a monitoring service. If you intend to add remote monitoring, confirm RTSP support before purchasing. Ask the supplier directly or check the technical specification sheet — don't assume.

RTSP-capable 4G solar cameras exist from reputable manufacturers — they're just not always the cheapest or most prominently marketed option. It's worth the extra due diligence upfront.

Audio Deterrence

IP speakers with two-way audio and strobe sirens add an active deterrent layer. When a monitoring operator confirms a real intrusion, they can issue a verbal warning through the speaker or activate a siren — often enough to cause someone to leave the site without taking anything. This is part of what SecurifyHQ's Deter plan includes.

Remote Monitoring

Remote monitoring means that when AI detects a person on your site after hours, a human operator reviews the footage in near real-time. If the event is confirmed as suspicious activity, your nominated escalation contacts are notified according to your pre-authorised instructions. This may include calling a licensed security response company, contacting police, or alerting you directly.

The key difference from a recording-only camera setup: there's a human in the loop who can act while the event is still happening, not the following morning.

Monitoring vs. Insurance: Do You Need Both?

Insurance covers the financial cost of theft after it has occurred. Monitoring is aimed at detection and response while it's happening — or shortening the window in which theft can occur undetected.

They serve different purposes. For sites with repeated incidents, or where a single theft event could cost tens of thousands of dollars, remote monitoring may reduce both the frequency of incidents and the cost of claims. Your insurer may also want to see evidence of active security measures when assessing premiums or claims — documented monitoring records can support this.

The question to ask is: what's the cost of a single significant theft event compared to the cost of monitoring over the build period? For most commercial construction projects, the numbers favour adding monitoring.
