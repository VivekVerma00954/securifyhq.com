Why Motion Sensors Make Camera Monitoring Far More Effective

Cameras capture what happens on your site. Sensors detect that something is happening in the first place. For businesses that want more than a recording system — that want an actual response capability — combining both creates a meaningfully different level of protection.

This article explains what motion sensors do, why they work differently to cameras, and why SecurifyHQ includes sensor monitoring as a core feature of its SHQ Pro and SHQ Pro Plus plans.

What Cameras Do Well — and Where They Fall Short

A well-placed IP camera does several things well. It provides a visual record of what happened on your site. It deters opportunistic behaviour — people who see cameras are less likely to act. And when connected to a verified monitoring service like SecurifyHQ, it gives a human operator something to actually look at when an alert fires.

But cameras are reactive tools. They capture what they're pointed at, continuously or when motion is detected. The problem is that motion detection on cameras is notoriously unreliable as a trigger for human review: wind-blown branches, cars passing on a nearby road, lighting changes, insects on the lens — all of these generate alerts that operators have to filter through. Over time, high false-alert rates erode response quality.

There's also the question of coverage. No camera setup covers every possible entry point at every angle. Skylights, rear doors, internal corridors, secure rooms — any space where a camera isn't positioned is a gap that only an intruder will discover.

What Motion Sensors Add

PIR motion sensors, door/window contact sensors, and vibration detectors work on a different principle to cameras. They don't produce footage — they produce events. When someone crosses a PIR zone, a door is opened, or a surface is struck, the sensor fires a signal. That signal arrives at SecurifyHQ's monitoring system in near real-time, independently of what any camera is doing.

This matters for three reasons:

  • Speed: A sensor event reaches our system before a camera clip can be reviewed. The alert is already being acted on while the operator is pulling up the relevant footage.
  • Confidence: A sensor event corroborated by camera footage gives an operator much higher confidence that something real is happening — not a false positive. This confidence is what enables faster, more decisive escalation.
  • Coverage: Sensors cover spaces that cameras don't. A vibration sensor on a roller door or a contact sensor on a pedestrian gate catches access attempts that may not be visible on any camera feed.

The Three Sensor Types We Use

🔍 PIR Motion Sensor

Detects body heat movement within a defined zone. Ideal for open interior areas, corridors, and blind spots between cameras. Fires instantly on human presence.

🚪 Door / Window Contact

Fires when a door or window is opened. Placed on entry points, roller doors, or internal secure areas. Simple, reliable, and produces near-zero false positives.

📳 Vibration Sensor

Detects impact on surfaces — typically applied to roller doors, walls, or glass. Catches forced entry attempts before access is achieved.

All three sensor types use Zigbee wireless communication, so installation doesn't require cabling runs. Sensors connect to a small on-site hub and report to SecurifyHQ's monitoring system via the same secure VPN connection used by the cameras.

How Dual-Layer Detection Works in Practice

Here's a realistic sequence of events at a monitored site:

  1. 10:47pm: A vibration sensor on the rear roller door fires. The event reaches SecurifyHQ's system immediately.
  2. 10:47pm: The operator is alerted and pulls up the rear camera feed. They can see someone at the roller door.
  3. 10:48pm: A PIR sensor inside the loading bay fires — the door has been breached. The operator now has two corroborating signals and visible footage of a person inside.
  4. 10:48pm: The operator activates the pre-authorised escalation sequence: alert strobe fires, audio deterrent activates, and your nominated contact is called.

Without sensors, step 1 doesn't happen. The operator may eventually notice motion on a camera — but the response is slower, and the confidence level is lower. With sensors, the alert is immediate and contextualised before the operator even looks at footage.

Why we verify before acting: SecurifyHQ never escalates based on a sensor event alone. Human verification — an operator reviewing what the cameras show — happens first. This prevents false activations and ensures escalation is proportionate to what's actually happening.

Where Sensors Are Most Valuable

Not every site needs all three sensor types. We work with clients during setup to identify the highest-priority detection points based on their layout and past incidents. Common priorities include:

  • Roller doors and rear vehicle access points (vibration sensors)
  • Pedestrian entry doors and gates (contact sensors)
  • Internal corridors leading to high-value stock areas (PIR sensors)
  • Secure rooms — server rooms, cash offices, drug storage (PIR + contact combination)
  • Areas with camera blind spots (PIR sensors to cover the gap)

For sites with robust camera coverage already in place, adding even a small number of strategically placed sensors significantly improves the speed and confidence of the monitoring response.
