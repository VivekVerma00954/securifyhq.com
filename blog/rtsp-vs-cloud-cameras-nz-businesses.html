RTSP vs. Cloud Cameras: What NZ Businesses Need to Know Before Buying

Choosing the wrong security camera can be a costly mistake — not because the camera fails to record, but because it locks you out of your own footage and prevents you from adding professional monitoring later. Before you purchase cameras for your business, it's worth understanding the fundamental difference between cloud-locked cameras and RTSP-capable cameras.

What Are Cloud Cameras?

Cloud cameras — sold by brands like Arlo, Eufy, Ring, and many basic 4G solar camera manufacturers — send footage directly to the vendor's cloud servers. You access your footage through the vendor's app. That's it.

This works well for basic home use. For businesses with more specific needs, the limitations become apparent quickly:

  • Your footage lives on the vendor's servers, not yours
  • The stream can't be accessed by third-party systems or monitoring services
  • You're dependent on the vendor's subscription plans and pricing
  • If the vendor changes their app, discontinues the product, or increases fees, you have limited options
  • Integration with NVRs, alarm systems, or professional monitoring platforms is typically not possible

Cloud cameras are convenient out of the box. But that convenience comes with a trade-off: vendor dependency.

What Are RTSP Cameras?

RTSP stands for Real Time Streaming Protocol — an open standard that allows a camera to broadcast its video feed to any compatible system. An RTSP-capable camera is hardware you control. You can point its stream at an NVR, a monitoring service, AI detection software, or any other platform that accepts RTSP input.

Most business-grade IP cameras support RTSP. Common compatible brands include:

  • Hikvision — widely available in NZ, good image quality across price points
  • Dahua — strong feature set, popular in commercial installations
  • Reolink — more affordable, many models support RTSP (verify per model)
  • Axis — premium tier, highly reliable, used in enterprise settings
  • Uniview — solid mid-range option with good NZ availability

RTSP cameras require a bit more setup than plug-in cloud cameras, but they give you full control over where your footage goes and how it's used.

Why RTSP Matters If You Want to Add Monitoring Later

If you install cloud-only cameras today and later decide you want AI-powered remote monitoring, you'll likely need to replace your hardware. The monitoring service cannot access a cloud-locked camera's stream — there's simply no way in.

With RTSP cameras, adding professional monitoring is straightforward: the monitoring service connects to your camera's stream over a secure VPN connection. No new hardware required in most cases.

This is worth thinking about at the point of purchase. Cameras are a capital investment that often stay in place for five to ten years. Buying hardware that locks you in now limits your options later.

A Note on 4G Solar Cameras

Solar-powered 4G cameras have become popular for construction sites, rural properties, and remote locations where cabled internet isn't available. Many of these cameras are sold through general trade suppliers and online marketplaces.

Important: Not all 4G solar cameras support RTSP. Many are cloud-only devices that send footage to a proprietary app. Before purchasing any 4G solar camera for a site where you intend to add monitoring, confirm explicitly with the supplier that the camera outputs an RTSP stream. Don't assume — check the spec sheet or contact the manufacturer.

There are 4G solar cameras that do support RTSP, but they're not always marketed with that feature front and centre. It pays to ask before you buy.

Practical Advice Before You Buy

Here's a simple checklist to work through when evaluating security cameras for your business:

  • Does it support RTSP? Look for this in the spec sheet or product description. If it's not mentioned, ask the supplier directly.
  • Can it connect to an NVR or DVR? This is a reasonable proxy for RTSP compatibility, though not guaranteed — confirm both.
  • Who stores the footage? If the answer is "the vendor's cloud," understand what that means for your data sovereignty and ongoing costs.
  • Is the camera on a standard IP network? Cameras on proprietary wireless systems (like some Arlo mesh networks) may not behave like standard IP cameras.
  • What's the plan if the vendor changes pricing or discontinues the product? With RTSP cameras pointed at your own NVR or a monitoring service, this risk is much lower.
