5 Signs Your NZ Business Security System Is Failing You

From unreviewed footage to false alarm fatigue, here are 5 signs your current CCTV setup isn't doing its job — and what to do about it.

← Back to Blog

Most NZ businesses that invest in CCTV do so with good intentions. They want to protect their stock, their staff, and their property. But having cameras installed is very different from having a security system that actually works. In practice, a large number of commercial CCTV setups are providing a false sense of protection — capturing everything faithfully while doing nothing to prevent or respond to incidents.

Here are five clear signs that your current setup is letting you down — and what a properly monitored system looks like instead.

Sign 1

Nobody Is Reviewing the Footage

This is the most common failure and the most consequential. If your cameras record continuously but nobody watches the footage until after an incident, you don't have a security system — you have a very expensive archive. Footage reviewed after a break-in tells you what happened. It does nothing to stop it, limit the damage, or speed up response. If your honest answer to "who watches the cameras?" is "nobody, unless something happens," that's the sign your setup is passive rather than protective.

Sign 2

Your Alarm System Causes Fatigue, Not Action

False alarms are one of the most damaging security problems a business can have — not because they're costly on their own, but because of what they train people to do. When staff, owners, or security contacts receive repeated alerts for cats, wind, shadows, or headlights, they start ignoring them. By the time a real event triggers the system, the response is slower, less urgent, or doesn't happen at all. If your team has learned to dismiss alerts as background noise, your alarm system has stopped functioning as a deterrent and started functioning as a distraction.

Sign 3

There Is No Escalation Plan

If an alert fires at 2am and your on-call contact doesn't answer, what happens next? For most businesses with self-managed camera systems, the honest answer is: nothing. There's no second contact to call, no guard company on standby, no documented procedure for what happens when the first line of response fails. An escalation plan — a pre-agreed sequence of contacts and actions that activates automatically when an alert isn't acknowledged — is the difference between a monitored site and a recorded one. Without it, you're one unanswered phone call away from a completely uncontrolled incident.

Sign 4

You Have No Incident Reports

After every verified security event, there should be a structured record: what was detected, at what time, what the operator saw, what actions were taken, and what the outcome was. If your current system produces no documentation beyond raw video files, you're missing a critical layer of value. Incident reports support insurance claims, give investigators something concrete to work with, help you identify patterns of activity at your site, and demonstrate to insurers that your security is actively managed. The absence of structured reporting is a sign that your system is passive — it records, but it doesn't document or escalate.

Sign 5

Your Site Has No After-Hours Coverage

The majority of commercial break-ins in New Zealand occur outside business hours — evenings, weekends, and public holidays. These are exactly the periods when most self-managed camera systems are effectively unwatched. Cameras record everything. But if nobody is monitoring those feeds during the hours your site is empty, the recording capability is irrelevant to prevention. Effective after-hours coverage means someone — or a monitored system with human oversight — is watching for activity during the periods when your site is most vulnerable and your response capability is at its lowest.
