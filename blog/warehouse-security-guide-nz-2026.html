Warehouse Security in New Zealand: A Practical Guide for 2026

Warehouses present a concentrated security challenge. Unlike retail stores with foot traffic throughout the day, warehouses typically hold large amounts of inventory in a space that's largely unoccupied after hours. That combination — high value, low visibility — makes them a consistent target.

This guide covers the most common vulnerabilities in NZ warehouse environments, what security measures actually help, and how to think about layering your approach so you're not relying on any single point of failure.

The Problem: High Value, Limited After-Hours Presence

The typical warehouse has concentrated inventory — pallets of stock, machinery, vehicles, raw materials — all in one location. During operating hours, staff are present and activity is visible. After 5pm, the building is largely empty. Incidents that occur overnight may not be discovered until the next morning, giving perpetrators hours to act without interruption.

Common incident types in NZ warehouse settings include:

  • After-hours break-ins — usually targeting perimeter access points: roller doors, pedestrian entry, skylights
  • Loading dock vulnerabilities — docks are often accessible from the rear of a property and out of sightlines from the street
  • Internal theft — harder to detect without systematic monitoring, often accumulates over time before being noticed
  • Opportunistic vehicle theft — forklifts, company vehicles, or trailers left in yards overnight

What CCTV Alone Doesn't Solve

Most warehouses in New Zealand have some CCTV installed. The problem isn't usually the absence of cameras — it's what happens (or doesn't happen) when something is recorded.

A camera that records an incident is useful for investigation afterward. It can support a police report or insurance claim. But it doesn't prevent the incident, and it doesn't trigger a response while the event is still happening.

This is the core limitation of recording-only CCTV: it's retrospective. You find out what happened after the fact, often the next morning when staff arrive. By that point, the damage is done.

A Layered Security Approach for Warehouses

Effective warehouse security isn't about finding one solution — it's about layering several that each address a different failure mode.

Physical Barriers and Access Control

Perimeter fencing, secure roller door locks, and access-controlled pedestrian entry are the first layer. They slow down or deter opportunistic intruders. Access control systems that log entry and exit events also provide useful records if internal theft becomes a concern.

Lighting

Well-lit yards and perimeter areas are underrated as a deterrent. Motion-activated lighting that illuminates when someone approaches an entry point makes a site less attractive to those relying on darkness for cover.

Camera Placement

Camera coverage should prioritise: all pedestrian and vehicle entry points, loading docks, internal high-value storage areas, and yard perimeters. Cameras positioned only in obvious locations (front entrance, main aisle) leave blind spots that experienced thieves will use.

Remote Monitoring

Remote monitoring adds a human review layer. When AI detects motion or a person after hours, a human operator reviews the clip in near real-time. If the event is assessed as genuine, they act on your pre-authorised escalation instructions — calling your nominated contacts, your security company, or emergency services as you've directed.

This is particularly relevant for warehouses because after-hours incidents are otherwise undetected until morning. Monitoring shortens that window considerably.

Clear Escalation Plan

Any monitoring arrangement is only as useful as the escalation plan behind it. Before setting up remote monitoring, document: who gets called first, who is the backup contact, whether you want a licensed guard response company involved, and what constitutes a situation requiring police contact. SecurifyHQ acts on your pre-authorised instructions — so those instructions need to exist before an incident occurs.

Practical Warehouse Security Checklist

  • Camera coverage at all entry points, loading docks, and yard perimeters
  • Cameras are RTSP-capable IP cameras (not cloud-locked devices)
  • Adequate lighting at all external access points and yards
  • Access control or key management system for after-hours entry
  • Roller door locks and perimeter fencing in serviceable condition
  • Written escalation plan: who to call, in what order, under what conditions
  • Regular review of access logs and camera footage (at least weekly)
  • Staff awareness of internal procedures for reporting suspicious activity

Note on connectivity: Remote monitoring requires your cameras to be reachable over the internet. For most warehouses with a fixed broadband connection, this is straightforward. When Does Remote Monitoring Make Sense?

Remote monitoring is most valuable when your warehouse is unoccupied for extended periods, when the value of your inventory makes a single incident genuinely costly, or when you've had previous incidents and want a more active response capability than recording-only cameras provide.

It's not a replacement for physical barriers and access control — it works alongside them. If someone is on your site after hours and motion is detected, monitoring provides a mechanism to identify what's happening and initiate a response based on what the footage shows.
